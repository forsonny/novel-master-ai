# Narrative Techniques Reference for Novel Writing

This guide provides a comprehensive reference for narrative techniques, story structures, and writing craft that Novel Master uses to generate and refine story elements.

## Story Structures

### Three-Act Structure
**Classic structure used in most commercial fiction**

**Act I (Setup): 25% of story**
- Hook: Opening scene that grabs attention
- Inciting Incident: Event that disrupts status quo
- Plot Point 1: Decision that propels into Act II

**Act II (Confrontation): 50% of story**
- Rising Action: Obstacles and complications
- Midpoint: Major turning point or revelation
- Crisis: Darkest moment, all seems lost
- Plot Point 2: Decision that leads to climax

**Act III (Resolution): 25% of story**
- Climax: Final confrontation
- Falling Action: Consequences and aftermath
- Resolution: New equilibrium established

### Hero's Journey (Monomyth)
**Common in fantasy and adventure**

1. **Ordinary World:** Hero's normal life
2. **Call to Adventure:** Challenge presented
3. **Refusal of Call:** Hero hesitates
4. **Meeting Mentor:** Guide appears
5. **Crossing Threshold:** Enters special world
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies:** Challenges and helpers
7. **Approach to Inmost Cave:** Preparation for ordeal
8. **Ordeal:** Greatest challenge
9. **Reward:** Gains something valuable
10. **Road Back:** Return journey begins
11. **Resurrection:** Final test
12. **Return with Elixir:** Transformed, brings wisdom

### Save the Cat Beat Sheet
**Popular screenwriting structure adapted for novels**

1. **Opening Image (1%):** Visual that represents the whole
2. **Theme Stated (5%):** What story is about
3. **Setup (1-10%):** World and character introduction
4. **Catalyst (10%):** Inciting incident
5. **Debate (10-20%):** Should hero proceed?
6. **Break into Two (20%):** Act II begins
7. **B Story (22%):** Secondary plotline
8. **Fun and Games (20-50%):** Promise of premise
9. **Midpoint (50%):** False victory or defeat
10. **Bad Guys Close In (50-75%):** Opposition strengthens
11. **All Is Lost (75%):** Darkest moment
12. **Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%):** Hero reflects
13. **Break into Three (80%):** Act III begins
14. **Finale (80-99%):** Climax and resolution
15. **Final Image (99-100%):** Contrast with opening

### Seven-Point Story Structure
**Character-focused structure**

1. **Hook:** Character in opposite state
2. **Plot Turn 1:** Inciting incident
3. **Pinch Point 1:** Apply pressure
4. **Midpoint:** Character moves from reaction to action
5. **Pinch Point 2:** Apply more pressure
6. **Plot Turn 2:** Power to resolve
7. **Resolution:** Character in new state

## POV Techniques

### First Person
**Advantages:**
- Intimate connection with narrator
- Strong voice and personality
- Immediate emotional impact
- Unreliable narrator possibilities

**Challenges:**
- Limited to narrator's knowledge
- Can feel claustrophobic
- Harder to show other characters' thoughts

**Best For:**
- Character-driven stories
- Mysteries (detective POV)
- Coming-of-age stories
- Stories with unreliable narrators

### Third Person Limited
**Advantages:**
- Close to character but with flexibility
- Can switch POV between scenes
- Maintains some distance
- Most common in commercial fiction

**Challenges:**
- Must establish POV clearly
- Switching POVs can confuse
- Less intimate than first person

**Best For:**
- Most commercial fiction
- Multiple POV stories
- Stories needing flexibility

### Third Person Omniscient
**Advantages:**
- Can show any character's thoughts
- Can provide authorial commentary
- Epic scope
- Historical or literary fiction feel

**Challenges:**
- Can feel distant
- Harder to maintain tension
- Requires strong authorial voice

**Best For:**
- Epic fantasy
- Literary fiction
- Historical fiction
- Stories with large casts

### Multiple POV
**Best Practices:**
- Limit to 3-5 POV characters
- Use distinct voices for each
- Switch at chapter breaks
- Each POV should advance plot
- Balance screen time

## Pacing Techniques

### Scene and Sequel Structure
**Scene (Action):**
- Goal: What character wants
- Conflict: Obstacle in the way
- Disaster: Failure or complication

**Sequel (Reaction):**
- Reaction: Emotional response
- Dilemma: What to do next?
- Decision: New goal chosen

### Chapter Endings
**Techniques:**
- Cliffhanger: Suspenseful moment
- Revelation: Important information
- Emotional beat: Character moment
- Plot twist: Unexpected turn
- Question: Unanswered question

### Pacing Variation
- **Fast Pacing:** Short sentences, action, dialogue, present tense
- **Slow Pacing:** Long sentences, description, reflection, past tense
- **Rhythm:** Alternate fast and slow sections

### Tension and Release
- Build tension through conflict
- Release through resolution or humor
- Vary intensity throughout story
- Peak at climax

## Character Development

### Character Arc Types
1. **Positive Arc:** Character grows, overcomes flaw
2. **Negative Arc:** Character degrades, succumbs to flaw
3. **Flat Arc:** Character changes world, not themselves
4. **No Arc:** Character remains same (rare, usually secondary)

### Character Dimensions
- **Surface:** What others see
- **Depth:** Internal thoughts and feelings
- **Backstory:** Past experiences
- **Motivation:** What drives them
- **Goal:** What they want
- **Conflict:** What stands in their way

### Dialogue Techniques
- **Voice:** Distinct speech patterns
- **Subtext:** What's not being said
- **Purpose:** Advance plot or reveal character
- **Conflict:** Even friendly dialogue has tension
- **Pacing:** Vary dialogue with action

## World-Building Techniques

### Show, Don't Tell
- Reveal world through character actions
- Use sensory details
- Let readers discover naturally
- Avoid info dumps

### Consistency Rules
- Establish rules early
- Document important rules
- Check for contradictions
- Maintain internal logic

### Layered Revelation
- Start with surface details
- Reveal deeper layers gradually
- Save major reveals for key moments
- Build mystery and intrigue

## Emotional Beats

### Emotional Arc
1. **Setup:** Establish emotional baseline
2. **Rising Emotion:** Build intensity
3. **Peak:** Emotional climax
4. **Release:** Emotional resolution
5. **New Baseline:** Changed emotional state

### Emotional Techniques
- **Sensory Details:** Physical sensations
- **Internal Monologue:** Character thoughts
- **Body Language:** Physical reactions
- **Metaphor/Simile:** Emotional comparisons
- **Pacing:** Match emotion to pacing

## Revision Techniques

### Macro Revision
- **Structure:** Act structure, pacing
- **Character:** Arcs, consistency, voice
- **Plot:** Logic, tension, resolution
- **Theme:** Clarity, development

### Micro Revision
- **Prose:** Sentence structure, word choice
- **Dialogue:** Voice, subtext, purpose
- **Description:** Sensory details, specificity
- **Pacing:** Sentence length, scene length

### Continuity Checks
- **Character:** Appearance, voice, motivation
- **Timeline:** Chronology, time passage
- **World:** Rules, consistency
- **Plot:** Logic, cause and effect
